I've seen teams that looked fine on paper fall apart in slow motion. Velocity
was up, features were shipping, the brilliant engineer in the corner was closing
tickets faster than anyone else. And yet, one by one, people started leaving.
First the quieter ones, then the more senior folks who had options. By the time
the pattern was obvious, the damage was done.

The common thread every time: a brilliant jerk who nobody wanted to deal with.

The term gets thrown around loosely, so let me be precise about what I mean. A
brilliant jerk is someone who is genuinely technically excellent — not just
competent, but exceptional — and who is also consistently destructive to the
people around them. Condescending in code reviews, dismissive in meetings,
territorial about their work, quick to humiliate and slow to collaborate. The
combination is what makes them dangerous. If they weren't brilliant, the
behavior would be easy to act on. If they weren't jerks, there'd be nothing to
write about.

## Why they survive so long

The rationalization writes itself. They ship more than anyone else. Their
architecture decisions have saved the company twice. Replacing them would set
the roadmap back by months. And besides, they're just... direct. Maybe a bit
rough around the edges. People need to toughen up.

This is almost always how it goes. The individual output is visible and easy to
measure. The damage they do is diffuse, slow, and measured in things that don't
show up in a sprint report — the question the junior engineer didn't ask because
they didn't want to get publicly demolished, the design doc that never got
written because the author knew it would get torn apart without a fair hearing,
the senior hire who took a counteroffer after one too many meetings.

Leadership also often has a conflict-avoidance problem. Confronting a brilliant
jerk is unpleasant, time-consuming, and uncertain. The outcome of doing nothing
feels known — the tickets keep closing. The outcome of acting feels risky. So
nothing happens, and the calculus silently shifts: the team starts to organize
itself around avoiding this person instead of doing its best work.

That's the real cost. Not the jerk's behavior in isolation. The behavioral
adaptation of everyone else.

## The hidden bill

Once you understand that the team is adapting to the jerk rather than to the
work, the costs become easier to see.

**Attrition is the most obvious one.** Your best people leave first because they
have the most options and the least tolerance for nonsense. The people who stay
are increasingly those who can't leave or have learned to look the other way.
Over time, you're selecting for the wrong traits.

**Knowledge siloing is the second.** Brilliant jerks rarely document, rarely
mentor, and rarely welcome questions. Their knowledge is their power and they
know it. When they eventually leave — and they always do, on their timeline, not
yours — they take years of context with them and leave a crater.

**Psychological safety collapses.** I've written about [Tuckman's
stages]({% post_url 2025-11-01-tuckman-stages %}) before — a team can be stuck
in Storming indefinitely when one person makes it unsafe to disagree. You never
get to Norming. The norming stage requires people to express opinions openly,
and that requires trust. A brilliant jerk in the room poisons that trust at the
source.

**The hiring pipeline suffers.** Strong candidates talk to the team during the
interview process. Word gets around. You'll find yourself losing candidates you
wanted and keeping ones you should have passed on, not because your process is
broken but because your reputation is.

All of this is real cost. It just doesn't show up next to the jerk's JIRA
metrics.

## The startup multiplier

In a large company, a brilliant jerk can be managed through org structure. Put
them on solo work, keep them away from junior engineers, let them do their thing
in a corner. It's not great, but the blast radius is contained.

In a startup, there is no such containment. A ten-person team has no corners.
Everyone is in every meeting, every code review, every post-mortem. One person's
behavior affects every other person's experience of coming to work.

The trust that small teams run on is extraordinarily fragile. You don't have
process, you don't have layers of management, you don't have HR machinery to
absorb friction. You have a group of people who have chosen to bet their time on
something hard, and that bet requires believing in each other. A brilliant jerk
corrodes that belief faster than any product setback or funding scare.

Startups are also where the "we can't afford to lose them" argument is most
seductive and most wrong. The dependency on one person's technical output is
already a risk — it means the knowledge isn't distributed, the system isn't
legible, the team isn't resilient. The brilliant jerk didn't create this
situation by accident. They tend to cultivate it.

## False positives

A quick note before the prescription: not every intense, demanding, or blunt
engineer is a brilliant jerk. It's worth being clear about the distinction.

Someone who sets a high bar in code reviews isn't a jerk — they're raising the
standard. Someone who pushes back hard on a design decision isn't a jerk —
they're engaged. Someone who is brusque under deadline pressure isn't
necessarily a jerk — they're stressed, and that's manageable.

The line is intent and consistency. A brilliant jerk is destructive _toward
people_, not just demanding about _work_. They belittle, they exclude, they make
people feel stupid for asking questions. They do it not occasionally under
stress but habitually, as a mode of operating. And critically, they don't
improve when the behavior is named. They explain why the other person is wrong
to be bothered.

Don't confuse "hard to work with on this specific thing" with "systematically
destructive to everyone around them." The former is a conversation. The latter
is a decision.

## What to do about it

First, name it. This is harder than it sounds. The tendency is to describe the
behavior in softer terms — "communication style issues," "needs to work on
collaboration," "can be a bit much." That framing protects nobody. If you're a
manager and someone on your team is consistently making other people's
professional lives worse, that's not a style issue. Name it clearly, to yourself
first.

Second, have the direct conversation. Once and seriously, with specific
examples, with explicit expectations about what changes, and with a real
timeline. Not as a formality to check a box, but as a genuine attempt to see if
the behavior can shift. Some people don't realize how they're landing. A small
number will actually change when confronted with clarity. Most won't — but you
owe the attempt, and you need to have made it.

Third, stop rationalizing the output. This is the hardest part. You have to
actually believe that a team without this person, over any meaningful time
horizon, will outperform a team with them. The short-term productivity hit of
losing someone brilliant is real. The long-term cost of keeping them is almost
always larger, and it compounds. Run the math honestly.

If the behavior doesn't change after a clear conversation and a real timeline,
act on it. The decision doesn't get easier the longer you wait. Every week you
delay, someone else is quietly updating their resume.

I'd love to hear how others have navigated this — it's one of those problems
where there's no clean answer, only hard tradeoffs.
